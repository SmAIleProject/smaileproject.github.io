The AI Dilemma: A Classroom Debate on Ethics and Technology

SMaiLE Project

Target Group: 13-16 y.o.

Activity Duration: 60 min

Key Learning Goal: Empower participants to think critically and ethically about Artificial Intelligence by developing research, communication, and collaboration skills, enabling them to engage thoughtfully in societal discussions about technology.

Learning Outcomes

Students will be able to:

KNOWLEDGE & UNDERSTANDING:

  • Define basic AI concepts (e.g., machine learning, algorithms, chatbots) in simple terms.
  • Identify potential benefits and risks associated with AI in different contexts (ethics, education, daily life).
  • Explain different ethical viewpoints on AI-related issues.
  • Describe real-world examples of how AI is used in their lives.

SKILLS & ABILITIES:

  • Conduct basic research using age-appropriate sources (e.g., online articles, educational videos).
  • Formulate arguments supported by evidence.
  • Present arguments clearly and persuasively.
  • Listen attentively to opposing viewpoints and respond respectfully.
  • Work effectively with a team to develop a debate strategy.
  • Distinguish between facts and opinions.
  • Identify potential biases in information sources.

ATTITUDES & VALUES:

  • Demonstrate curiosity about technology and its impact on society.
  • Value the importance of ethical considerations in AI development.
  • Recognize the need for responsible use of AI.
  • Appreciate the value of diverse perspectives on complex issues.
  • Develop confidence in expressing their own opinions and ideas.

European Dimension / Erasmus+ Connection

Focus on EU Values

Students will be able to:

  • Frame debate topics to align with core EU values such as democracy, human rights, and social inclusion, considering how AI might impact these values.
  • Discuss the EU's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) and how Al systems should comply with its principles.
  • Explore how AI can promote multilingualism and intercultural understanding.
  • Debate the role of AI in combating misinformation within the EU.

Comparative Perspectives

Students will be able to:

  • Research and compare different approaches to Al regulation and development in various European countries, including national AI strategies and ethical guidelines.
  • Investigate how AI is being used in education systems across Europe.

European Examples

Students will be able to:

  • Use real-world examples of AI initiatives within the EU, such as EU-funded research projects, AI in public services (healthcare, transport), and technologies from European companies.

EU Policy Context

Students will be able to:

  • Understand relevant EU policies and initiatives related to AI, such as the AI Act.

Erasmus+ Connection & Project Integration

Students will be able to:

  • Incorporate the debate into an Erasmus+ project focused on digital literacy, citizenship education, or ethical technology.
  • Partner with schools in other European countries to conduct joint debates, share research, and use tools for cross-border collaboration.

1. Resources and Tools

  • Research Materials: Age-appropriate articles, websites, videos, documentaries, books, and access to online databases and libraries.
  • Debate Materials: Debate topic outlines, note-taking templates, evidence organizers, a timer, and judging rubrics.
  • Presentation Tools: Whiteboard or projector, markers, computer with internet access, and presentation software (e.g., PowerPoint, Google Slides).
  • Collaboration Tools: Online platforms for communication and collaboration (e.g., Google Docs, Microsoft Teams, Padlet) and shared document repositories.
  • EU-Specific Resources: Official EU publications on AI policy, websites of European AI research institutions, and news articles about Al in Europe.

2. Working Methods

  • Team-Based Learning: Divide students into teams to work collaboratively on research, argument development, and presentation preparation.
  • Inquiry-Based Learning: Encourage students to ask questions, explore different perspectives, and conduct independent research.
  • Active Learning: Use a variety of active learning techniques such as brainstorming, group discussions, and role-playing to engage students in the debate process.
  • Differentiated Instruction: Provide differentiated support and resources to meet the diverse learning needs of students.

3. Introduction and Motivation

Welcome to our exciting debate session on Artificial Intelligence (AI) and ethics! Today, we dive into a world where technology meets human values. As our planet becomes more connected and technologically advanced, AI plays a significant role in shaping our daily lives, from virtual assistants to autonomous vehicles. However, with great advancements come great responsibilities. The ethical implications of AI-how we should use it responsibly, ensure fairness, and protect privacy are crucial topics of discussion. This debate will allow you to explore these important issues, develop your own opinions, and learn how to express them clearly and persuasively.

4. Research and Learning

Students will be divided into teams to research and debate one of the following topics. Each topic includes a scenario, thought-provoking questions, and arguments to guide research.

Topic 4.1: AI in Education

Activity 1: School Surveillance "Should AI be used to monitor students in schools?"

It is the year 2035. Schools are using Al-powered surveillance to monitor students via cameras, facial recognition, and behavioral analysis to detect attention levels, rule adherence, and even stress. Some believe this improves safety, but others fear it invades privacy and could lead to unfair punishments.

  • How would you feel if cameras were constantly watching your face and body language?
  • Would you behave differently if you knew an Al was tracking your focus?
  • What could go wrong if the AI makes a mistake?
Arguments For Arguments Against
AI can detect fights, bullying, or dangerous behavior. Students are always being watched.
Shows who's paying attention or needs help. Constant surveillance can make students anxious.
Students behave better when they know they're being watched. It may misread behavior or emotions.
Can notice stress or sadness early. Teachers should decide not a machine.

Activity 2: AI Tools in Class - "Should schools teach students how to use AI tools?"

You remember ChatGPT while stuck on homework. It gives a clear answer in seconds. Some students already use Al to study smarter, but others copy without thinking. Teachers are split: some see AI as the future, others as a shortcut to lazy learning. Should schools ban AI, or teach students how to use it responsibly?

  • Does using AI make students smarter, or just better at copying?
  • Could relying on AI tools make your brain lazy over time?
  • What happens to students who don't have access to AI tools?
Arguments For Arguments Against
Schools should teach correct AI use since students already use it. Students might become too dependent on ΑΙ.
AI prepares students for future jobs and helps them learn better. Risk of solving problems without understanding.
It's better to teach ethical use than to pretend AI doesn't exist. AI can give wrong or biased answers.
Teaching AI in school ensures all students have the same chance to learn it. Not every school is ready, and teachers may not be trained.

Topic 4.2: AI and Ethics

Activity 1: AI in Medicine - "Should decisions about patient care be made by AI or by human doctors?"

Imagine a robot scans your symptoms, checks your records, and instantly gives a diagnosis and prescription before you even see a doctor. Would you trust it? Can Al make better decisions than tired, overworked doctors?

  • What happens if the AI makes a mistake? How does it learn?
  • Is it fair if only some people get access to high-tech care?
Arguments For Arguments Against
AI can analyze huge amounts of data quickly. AI can make mistakes or miss context.
AI doesn't get tired, emotional, or distracted. Doctors understand patient emotions and can show empathy.
Faster diagnoses can save lives, especially where there are not enough doctors. AI might be biased depending on its data.
Human judgment is needed for complex cases.

5. Creative Application

Team Policy Debate Format

The creative application is a structured team policy debate. The class is divided into an Affirmative Team (arguing for the policy change), a Negative Team (defending the status quo), Judges, and an Audience. The audience can participate via a shared Padlet board. The debate follows a strict format and timing:

  • Preparation Time: Teams have 8-10 minutes total to use as they see fit.
  • Constructive Speeches (2 min each): Each team presents its primary case.
    1. 1st Affirmative Constructive (1AC)
    2. 1st Negative Constructive (1NC)
    3. 2nd Affirmative Constructive (2AC)
    4. 2nd Negative Constructive (2NC)
  • Cross-Examinations (3 min each): Debaters question the opposing speaker.
    1. 2nd Negative questions 1st Affirmative
    2. 1st Affirmative questions 1st Negative
    3. 1st Negative questions 2nd Affirmative
    4. 2nd Affirmative questions 2nd Negative
  • Rebuttal Speeches (5 min each): Teams refute opponent's arguments and reinforce their own case. No new arguments are introduced.
    1. 1st Negative Rebuttal (1NR)
    2. 1st Affirmative Rebuttal (1AR)
    3. 2nd Negative Rebuttal (2NR)
    4. 2nd Affirmative Rebuttal (2AR)

6. Reflection and Evaluation

Judging Rubric

Judges will evaluate debates based on the criteria outlined below. The audience can also vote for the team with the most convincing arguments.

Criteria Weight Description
Arguments 30% Quality and clarity of arguments. Use of evidence to support claims. Addresses key issues related to the topic.
Research & Evidence 25% Demonstrated understanding of the topic. Use of relevant and credible evidence to support arguments. Sources are cited appropriately.
Delivery 20% Clarity of speech and organization of presentation. Eye contact and engagement with the audience. Persuasiveness.
Rebuttals & Response 15% Ability to effectively address and respond to the opposing team's arguments. Demonstrates active listening and critical thinking.
Teamwork 10% Evidence of collaboration and support within the team. Speakers build upon each other's arguments. Effective use of time.

Final Reflection

The workshop concludes with feedback and reflection. This includes peer feedback, self-assessment, and a teacher-led class discussion. Students can use digital tools like Padlet or Google Forms for a final reflection on their learning.
